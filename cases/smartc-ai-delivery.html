Executive summary

SmartC was a technical delivery and recovery case, not a clean greenfield implementation.

The work involved AI-enabled transportation safety, hardware, computer vision/software behavior, QA validation, field integration, and sponsor-controlled governance. Pavlo's role was to coordinate delivery across cross-functional workstreams, make ambiguity visible, structure pilot readiness, and support the transition from recovery/redesign toward scalable deployment.

Primary valueImplementation governanceTurn technical ambiguity into visible delivery decisions.
Delivery environmentAI-enabled transportation technologyHardware, computer vision/software, QA, integration, and field validation.
Hiring signalTechnical PM / Implementation PM fitEvidence of cross-functional coordination without overstating technical authorship.
Problem / recovery context

Reliability and readiness gaps created delivery risk.

This was not a simple launch sequence. The project required recovery discipline after reliability and readiness gaps made delivery risk visible. Multiple technical streams had to be brought into one shared operating model so sponsors and delivery teams could understand what was ready, what was blocked, and what required decision support.

RiskTechnical streams were interdependent.Hardware, computer vision/software, QA, field validation, and integration could not be managed as isolated tasks.
RiskReadiness was ambiguous.Delivery needed clearer gates for validation, pilot confidence, and sponsor-facing status.
RiskChange needed visibility.Sponsor-controlled decisions required transparent scope, issue, and readiness communication.
RiskScale transition required credibility.Recovery and redesign had to support movement toward pilot validation and broader deployment.
Pavlo's PM role

Structured the delivery path across technical workstreams.

Pavlo's contribution is positioned as delivery leadership and implementation control: structuring workstreams, maintaining visibility over scope, risks, issues, dependencies, and readiness gaps, coordinating delivery rhythm, supporting sponsor decisions, and helping move the project toward pilot readiness and scale transition.

Owned / coordinated

  • Delivery rhythm and workstream visibility
  • RAID-style risk, issue, and dependency tracking
  • Sponsor-facing status and readiness communication
  • Milestone and QA-readiness tracking
  • Pilot-readiness structure and handoff visibility

Technical ownership boundaries

  • Engineering the AI model
  • Designing computer-vision algorithms
  • Owning hardware architecture
  • Claiming product invention
  • Replacing the technical team or domain specialists
