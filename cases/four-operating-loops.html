Prepaid-to-Consignment Operating Model Transformation

Following the client’s decision to move a major aerospace account from prepaid distribution to consignment, I served as the dedicated on-site implementation owner required by the contract. Over roughly 11 months, I translated the commercial model into the physical inventory, data, vending, replenishment, receiving, billing-attribution, and service-control system needed to operate it while production support continued.

01 / Transformation mandate

The account did not need a better delivery routine. It needed a new operating model.

The client initiated the shift to consignment and account leadership supported it. The commercial decision sat above my role; the implementation requirement sat directly with me. The new contract required a full-time MSC representative on site, and the operating model had to be built while daily production-support work continued.

At the start, usable consumption history was effectively absent. Inventory ownership, physical storage, vending configuration, item descriptions, user attribution, replenishment, receiving, and issue follow-up all had to be re-established around the new ownership model rather than copied from the prepaid process.

Legacy model

Prepaid, person-dependent distribution.

  • Client purchased inventory before consumption
  • Approximately 6 vending systems plus manual physical distribution
  • Remaining stock could be held locally but was already client-owned
  • Replenishment relied heavily on experience, forecast filtering, and manual checking
  • Paper item references and fragmented descriptions limited searchability
Target model

Consignment with controlled ownership, usage, and replenishment.

  • MSC inventory remained consigned until client consumption
  • 17 vending systems after 11 additional installations
  • Central hub, physical stockroom, numbered locations, and digital item structure
  • Users mapped to departments so consumption could follow decentralized budgets
  • Receiving, issue, transition, and replenishment controls supported the new model
02 / Implementation journey

The transformation was delivered as connected workstreams, not a single system launch.

Because the site could not stop operating, the implementation moved from physical control and data visibility into user attribution, service governance, replenishment automation, and stabilization. Each later control depended on the earlier layers being trustworthy.

Workstream 1

Establish physical control

Completed inventory work, expanded the vending estate from roughly 6 to 17 systems, established a central computer/hub and physical stockroom, and assigned numbered storage locations.

Workstream 2

Build the data foundation

Created the site item/location structure, expanded and standardized the catalog, and digitized legacy paper references so historic names and client terminology remained searchable.

Workstream 3

Configure users and consumption attribution

Set up vending users and mapped them to departments so material became client consumption at dispense and could align with decentralized departmental budgets.

Workstream 4

Create service and receiving governance

Introduced issue tracking, delivery and closure timing, ownership, escalation, and receiving logs to reconcile what arrived on site with records maintained elsewhere.

Workstream 5

Replace manual replenishment with decision logic

Moved from multi-hour forecast copying and cross-checking to an exception workflow using usage, lead time, MOQ/package constraints, inventory state, open orders, and human judgement.

Workstream 6

Control the prepaid-to-consignment transition

Built monitoring logic for legacy prepaid inventory so the handoff to consignment stock could be timed, billing stakeholders notified, forecast treatment updated, and premature duplicate replenishment avoided.

Workstream 7

Stabilize and optimize

Used feedback, ordering accuracy, exception patterns, slow-moving inventory analysis, and adjacent programs such as regrind control logic to improve the operating model after go-live.

03 / Operating control layer

Six control paths made the new model operable day to day.

The transformation created the operating model; these controls made it manageable. Each path answered a different management question around feedback, physical capacity, replenishment, lifecycle, supplier/specification gates, and shortage recovery.

Floor feedback became an operating signal

Informal production-floor concerns were converted into a visible review path with explicit ownership and follow-up.

Operating problem

Issues could remain anecdotal until they developed into shortage or service escalations.

Control introduced

A structured review signal connected item, location, impact, owner, status, and next action.

Management decision

Separate urgent recovery from recurring-pattern analysis so immediate service and systemic correction could proceed in parallel.

Business value

Earlier visibility, cleaner escalation, and less dependence on memory or informal conversations.

04 / Management decisions

The implementation worked because the decision model was defined before the automation.

I had broad operational autonomy over the site implementation, while the commercial decision and enterprise strategy remained above my role. The key delivery decisions were therefore about sequencing, control design, adoption, and how to make the new model workable under live constraints.

01

Build trustworthy data before scaling automation.

Physical inventory, locations, legacy descriptions, and item structure were stabilized before automated replenishment was treated as decision support.

02

Model ownership and consumption as different states.

Consigned stock remained MSC-owned until dispense, so inventory control, departmental consumption, and billing attribution had to be designed around the ownership transition.

03

Automate visibility, retain judgement.

The replenishment workflow surfaced exceptions and context; it did not replace production-impact judgement with an opaque formula.

04

Separate commercial and technical gates.

Supplier availability, acceptable specification, stakeholder approval, order state, and physical receipt became distinct decision states.

05

Reconcile site reality with external records.

Receiving logs established what arrived, when, and from whom so local stock movement could be checked against records maintained outside the site process.

06

Validate changes in short live cycles.

New logic moved through Kanban testing, one-to-two-week feedback windows, ordering-accuracy checks, and adjustment before the old process was retired.

06 / Control economics

Not every control was a permanent fix; some were necessary transition mechanisms.

The consignment model exposed a practical distinction between controls that remove a root cause and controls that compensate for a transitional or external constraint. That distinction became important as the number of items, locations, and exceptions increased.

Root-cause controls

Change the operating condition itself.

  • Digitized item and location structure
  • Explicit lifecycle and uncertainty states
  • Separated supplier, specification, approval, order, and receipt states
  • Receiving records and structured issue ownership
Transition / compensating controls

Keep the model safe while another constraint remains.

  • Monitoring legacy prepaid stock until consignment takeover
  • Cross-reference logic where equivalent identities remained separate
  • Local automation bridging systems that did not provide one end-to-end decision view
  • Manual judgement retained for exceptions with insufficient evidence

A control remained valuable only while the operational risk it removed justified the maintenance, dependency, and coordination burden it introduced. Where repeated local controls only compensated for an externally owned constraint, the correct action was evidence-based escalation rather than endless local complexity.

07 / Scope & authority

The transformation scope was substantial; the authority boundary remained explicit.

Commercial strategy

The client requested the consignment model and account/C-level leadership supported the commercial direction. I did not originate or own that enterprise strategy.

Site implementation

My on-site role was required by the contract, and I had broad delegated autonomy to design, build, operate, test, and stabilize the processes needed to make the model function in practice.

Enterprise rollout

The account provides relevant evidence for a model intended to be more scalable for large clients, but rollout strategy for other accounts remained outside my ownership.
