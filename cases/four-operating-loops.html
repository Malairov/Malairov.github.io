Aerospace Service Delivery Controls

I built the operating foundation for a production-critical industrial supply program: structured data, exception-based planning, supplier and specification gates, issue ownership, lifecycle visibility, and shortage recovery.

01 / Mandate

Stabilize the work while building the system.

The program required daily service continuity, but its operating information was fragmented across portal exports, supplier communication, local knowledge, physical locations, and manual follow-up.

The mandate was not a clean-sheet software project. The system had to be constructed while orders, receipts, replenishment, stakeholder requests, shortages, and production-support issues continued in real time.

Before

Work existed, but the operating state was hard to see.

  • Fragmented item data and inconsistent descriptors
  • Manual review across large item volumes
  • Informal issue follow-up and unclear closure
  • Availability, approval, and ordering treated as one state
  • Limited evidence for recurring shortage analysis
After

A connected operating control system.

  • Structured master-data and cross-reference logic
  • Exception-based replenishment review
  • Explicit lifecycle, ownership, and escalation states
  • Separated supplier, specification, and approval gates
  • Measurable recovery and preventive follow-up
02 / Control system

Six operating paths replaced one undifferentiated stream of noise.

Each path answered a different management question. Select a control to see the operating problem, mechanism, decision logic, and business value.

Floor feedback became an operating signal

Informal production-floor concerns were converted into a visible review path with explicit ownership and follow-up.

Operating problem

Issues could remain anecdotal until they developed into shortage or service escalations.

Control introduced

A structured review signal connected item, location, impact, owner, status, and next action.

Management decision

Separate urgent recovery from recurring-pattern analysis so immediate service and systemic correction could proceed in parallel.

Business value

Earlier visibility, cleaner escalation, and less dependence on memory or informal conversations.

03 / Management decisions

The value was in the rules, not the spreadsheet.

Automation accelerated review only after the underlying management decisions were defined.

01

Automate visibility, retain judgement.

The workflow surfaced exceptions and context. It did not replace operational decisions with a formula that could not interpret production impact.

02

Separate commercial and technical gates.

Product availability, acceptable specification, stakeholder approval, order status, and physical receipt became distinct states.

03

Close the issue and the recurrence path.

Restoring supply resolved the event. A preventive action, owner, and evidence were required to address the repeating pattern.

04 / Results

Operational improvement tied to evidence.

The strongest result was not a single metric. It was a repeatable control layer that made scale, exceptions, ownership, and follow-up manageable.
