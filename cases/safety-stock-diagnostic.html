Case 03 · Deep dive · prepaid-to-consignment transformation

Replenishment Control Redesign

During an 11-month transition of a major aerospace account from prepaid supply to consignment, the site replenishment process had to move from person-dependent judgement and manual forecast review to a repeatable, data-driven operating model. This deep dive shows how I redesigned that decision chain, validated adoption, and then traced one hidden automation failure that could remove critical items before human review.

01 / Transformation context

The control redesign was one workstream inside a larger operating-model change.

The client initiated a move from prepaid supply to consignment, and the account required a dedicated full-time on-site implementation role. The commercial decision was made above my role; my responsibility was to turn that model into a functioning site operation while service continued. Stabilization took roughly eleven months, starting without usable consumption history.

Legacy operating model

Prepaid, person-dependent distribution.

  • Client owned inventory before consumption
  • A small initial point-of-use footprint plus manual distribution
  • Replenishment relied heavily on experience and forecast filtering
  • Paper item references and limited usable usage history
Consignment operating model

Controlled inventory, usage, replenishment, and accountability.

  • Consigned inventory remained supplier-owned until client consumption
  • 15+ automated point-of-use systems after substantial expansion
  • Central hub, physical stockroom, numbered locations, and digital item structure
  • User/department attribution, receiving controls, and data-driven replenishment

Scope boundary: I did not make the commercial decision to adopt consignment or own C-level account strategy. I had delegated autonomy for the site process and was the dedicated on-site role required to operationalize the new model.

02 / Failure

Automation solved the workload problem—and created a new decision risk.

Stable-item filtering reduced manual review demand, but its decision rule had a blind spot: insufficient history could be translated into a blank or neutral state and then filtered out. Every technical step could complete successfully while the resulting management decision was wrong.

The operational consequence was more serious than a spreadsheet defect: the decision surface could omit exactly the new items that required deliberate judgement, temporary protection, or stakeholder confirmation.

Misleading interpretation

No usage history equals no demand.

  • New item lacks mature demand history
  • Formula returns blank, zero, or neutral output
  • Classification treats the output as low priority
  • Filter removes the item from the review queue
Correct management interpretation

No history equals unresolved uncertainty.

  • Preserve the item as a distinct state
  • Assign a review reason and owner
  • Use temporary control logic where required
  • Close only after demand or disposition is known
03 / Decision log

The first decision was not to patch the visible formula.

The redesign started before the hidden filter defect was found. The core decision was to replace a 4–5 hour, person-dependent forecast review with a data-driven replenishment workflow based on usage, lead time, MOQ, inventory state, and explicit exceptions. When the new automation exposed a silent exclusion risk, I widened the diagnostic from a formula check to the full decision chain.

05 / Corrective design

Corrective controls were sequenced around dependency, not convenience.

Later checks were only useful if earlier states were trustworthy. The correction sequence therefore moved from state definition, to reconciliation, to structural hardening, scenario testing, and visible failure behaviour.

01

Explicit uncertainty state

New and sparse-history items received a visible classification rather than being forced into an established-demand model.

02

Stage reconciliation

Counts and keys were compared between source, transformed table, calculated state, macro output, and final review view.

03

Dynamic structure

Named headers, table objects, validation messages, and record checks replaced fragile fixed-column and fixed-range assumptions.

Sequence 1

Define valid states before automating decisions

Null, zero, sparse, and mature demand histories were separated so uncertainty could not be collapsed into a neutral result.

Sequence 2

Reconcile movement through the chain

Source records, transformed records, calculated states, macro output, and the final review view were checked against one another.

Sequence 3

Remove brittle structural assumptions

Named headers and table objects replaced fixed positions so a workbook change was less likely to create a silent mismatch.

Sequence 4

Test business scenarios, not only formulas

Four core demand-history states were tested through the decision chain, with abnormal states routed to visible review.

Sequence 5

Make structural failure visible

Missing headers, duplicate keys, reconciliation gaps, or unsupported states were designed to surface as errors rather than produce a silent partial result.

06 / Adoption & validation

The new logic was tested in live work, adjusted, and adopted as the normal site process.

The change was not treated as a one-time workbook deployment. New logic was moved into Kanban testing, observed for roughly one to two weeks, reviewed through team feedback and ordering-accuracy checks, and adjusted before becoming the normal replenishment path.

Pilot in live operations

Changes were tested against real replenishment work rather than accepted only because formulas refreshed successfully.

Measure and learn

Team feedback and ordering accuracy were used as acceptance signals, with a one-to-two-week observation window before the next adjustment.

Adopt and retire

The account replenishment workflow changed with the new process, and the old copy-filter-cross-check method was retired rather than maintained as a parallel fallback.

The result was an adopted replenishment process with a measurable control boundary.

Portfolio boundary: metrics are rounded representations of measured outcomes; client identity, product identifiers, supplier identities, pricing, transaction-level data, proprietary thresholds, and system-specific details are intentionally excluded. The broader legacy replenishment loop experienced multiple shortage events with several possible causes. This deep dive does not attribute all of those events to one filter defect or claim a quantified financial saving; it isolates one verified failure mechanism and the implemented control redesign around it.

08 / Control burden

The corrective design is stronger—but the extra controls are not free.

The original filter reduced review effort. Its blind spot then required explicit states, reconciliation, scenario tests, and visible-failure controls. The management question is therefore not simply whether to add more control, but whether each control removes more operational risk than permanent burden it introduces.

Benefit and permanent burden of the corrective controls
Control Benefit Permanent burden / residual risk
Explicit uncertainty state Prevents no-history items from being silently treated as no-demand items State definitions and transitions must remain aligned with business rules
Stage reconciliation Detects records lost or changed between stages Reconciliation logic must be maintained when the chain changes
Dynamic structure Reduces fixed-range and fixed-column brittleness Structural changes still require controlled validation
Scenario testing Tests the business decision across null, zero, sparse, and mature histories Scenarios must evolve when decision rules change
Visible failure Turns silent partial results into manageable exceptions More exceptions may require deliberate human resolution

Applied principle: a control remains valuable while the risk it removes is greater than the dependency and maintenance burden it creates. This case is a concrete example of one efficiency control generating a new failure mode and therefore requiring additional assurance controls.

09 / Scope boundary

This is transformation delivery at the client-account level—not a claim of enterprise-wide ownership.

Commercial decision sat above the role

The client requested the move to consignment and account/C-level leadership supported it. I did not originate or own that commercial strategy.

Site implementation was delegated

The new model required a dedicated site implementation role, with broad autonomy to build and operate the site process required to make consignment work in practice.

Technology followed policy constraints

Excel, Power Query, and VBA were used because approved tooling was constrained; the objective was not a software product but a controlled operating process that the site could actually use.
