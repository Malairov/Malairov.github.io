01 / Program context

A technical product needed delivery control, not another layer of activity.

The program combined computer vision, roadside hardware, edge computing, connectivity, monitoring, installation, environmental hardening, and sponsor acceptance. Technical progress existed, but readiness could not be inferred from activity alone.

My role was to turn concurrent specialist work into a decision-ready delivery system: define the baseline, expose dependencies, maintain risk and change visibility, connect testing evidence to readiness, and structure sponsor decisions.

Delivery remitI owned project planning, delivery governance, risk and dependency visibility, QA readiness, sponsor reporting, and pilot coordination. Engineering design and specialist technical decisions remained with the relevant technical leads.
Delivery risk

Progress could be reported without proving system readiness.

  • Hardware and software dependencies moved at different speeds
  • Defects, procurement, and sponsor changes competed for attention
  • Field conditions introduced risks not visible in laboratory work
  • Acceptance needed traceable evidence and decision ownership
Management response

Readiness became an explicit governance state.

  • Integrated schedule and milestone logic
  • RAID, action, defect, and dependency visibility
  • Defined QA and pilot-readiness gates
  • Change-impact analysis and sponsor decision packages

04 / Critical management episode

One baseline, live field conditions, different failure modes.

The city pilot validated one common MVP baseline under live field conditions. The management challenge was to preserve baseline comparability while the environment kept producing failure modes the lab never showed.

Baseline

Freeze the comparable core

Defined the functions and acceptance criteria that had to remain stable through the pilot, so field findings stayed comparable over time.

Night

Glare against the camera

At night, the LED warning sign interfered with camera detection. The resolution synchronized detection with the sign cycle — coordinated as a cross-workstream issue between the detection and hardware teams, with validation evidence before closure.

Winter

Freezing, cold-start, and enclosure

Heating, cold-start behaviour, and maintenance access were handled as controlled adaptations with separate acceptance evidence, without touching core product logic.

Visibility

Real-road visibility tuning

Camera placement, illumination, and projected-marking behaviour were tuned against real-road visibility conditions rather than test-bench assumptions.

Decision

Separate product defects from field adaptations

This preserved a clean sponsor view: what blocked the common baseline, what belonged to a site or environment package, and what could move into scale.
