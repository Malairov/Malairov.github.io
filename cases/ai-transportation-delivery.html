01 / Program context

Concurrent workstreams don't self-coordinate. Someone has to own the sequence.

The program combined computer vision, roadside hardware, edge computing, monitoring, installation, environmental hardening, and sponsor acceptance.

I turned concurrent specialist work into a decision-ready model covering baselines, dependencies, risk, change impacts, test records, readiness gates, and sponsor decisions.

Delivery remitI owned planning, risk and dependency tracking, QA gates, sponsor reporting, and pilot coordination. Engineering design stayed with technical leads.
Delivery risk

Progress could be reported before launch risk was clear.

  • Hardware, software, and procurement moved at different speeds
  • Field risks appeared beyond lab tests
  • Acceptance required records and decision ownership
Management response

Readiness became an explicit governance state.

  • Integrated schedule and milestone logic
  • RAID, action, defect, and dependency tracking
  • QA and pilot gates
  • Change-impact analysis for sponsor decisions

03 / Governance

Technical work became manageable through explicit routines.

01

Schedule and dependency management

Phase 1 used a Microsoft Project baseline; Phases 2 and 3 used Jira Scrum sprints. Procurement, QA, installation, and sponsor dependencies were mapped.

02

RAID, actions, and change governance

Risks, issues, assumptions, dependencies, defects, actions, and decisions were separated by owner. Change packages showed schedule, cost, risk, technical, and acceptance impact; the sponsor-controlled board kept the approval vote.

03

QA and pilot gates

Requirements, acceptance criteria, test records, defects, retest status, site status, and exceptions formed the go/no-go view.

04

Sponsor reporting and decisions

Weekly sponsor syncs translated technical detail into schedule or sprint state, risk, decisions, and next priorities.

04 / Critical management episode

One city pilot exposed several distinct field failure modes.

A common product baseline kept one sponsor view while product defects and site or environmental adaptations followed separate owners and acceptance paths.

Night

LED sign glare disrupted camera detection

Coordinated the issue until the technical team synchronized detection with the sign cycle, then linked the fix to retest records.

Wind

Camera displacement caused detection failure

Tracked the symptom to root cause, then coordinated the mounting correction and validation path.

Winter

Cabinet survival required formal adaptations

Heating, cold-start behaviour, enclosure protection, and maintenance became adaptation packages with acceptance records.

Decision

Separate product defects from site adaptations

This kept a clean sponsor picture of what blocked the baseline, what belonged to the site, and what could move.

06 / Transferability

Explicit gates make implementation credible.

Program governance

Built schedule, RAID, and change-control routines across vision, hardware, software, and installation teams working at different paces on different problems.

Field-to-sponsor translation

Converted defect status, retest records, and site conditions into weekly decisions a sponsor could act on without needing the technical detail underneath.

Phase-based delivery

Matched method to phase — Microsoft Project for the hardware baseline, Jira Scrum for software and monitoring sprints — rather than forcing one tool across dissimilar work.
