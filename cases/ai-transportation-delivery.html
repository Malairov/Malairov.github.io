01 / Program context

A technical product needed delivery control, not another layer of activity.

The program combined computer vision, roadside hardware, edge computing, monitoring, installation, environmental hardening, and sponsor acceptance. Activity alone did not prove readiness.

I converted concurrent specialist work into a decision-ready delivery system: baseline, dependencies, risk and change visibility, test evidence, readiness, and sponsor decisions.

Delivery remitI owned project planning, delivery governance, risk and dependency visibility, QA readiness, sponsor reporting, and pilot coordination. Engineering design and specialist technical decisions remained with the relevant technical leads.
Delivery risk

Progress could be reported without proving system readiness.

  • Hardware, software, and procurement moved at different speeds
  • Field conditions exposed risks not visible in laboratory work
  • Acceptance required traceable evidence and decision ownership
Management response

Readiness became an explicit governance state.

  • Integrated schedule and milestone logic
  • RAID, action, defect, and dependency visibility
  • Defined QA and pilot-readiness gates
  • Change-impact analysis and sponsor decision packages
02 / Delivery architecture

One system, five coordinated readiness states.

Detection and direction logic

Computer-vision workstreams identified pedestrians and movement direction. The delivery requirement was clear ownership, test evidence, dependency visibility, and readiness criteria across the technical team.

03 / Governance

Technical work became manageable through explicit controls.

01

Schedule and dependency control

Phase 1 used a Microsoft Project baseline; Phases 2 and 3 used Jira Scrum sprints with procurement, QA, installation, and sponsor dependencies visible.

02

RAID, actions, and change governance

Risks, issues, assumptions, dependencies, defects, actions, and decisions were separated by owner and response. Change packages showed schedule, cost, risk, technical, and acceptance impact; the sponsor-controlled board retained voting authority.

03

QA and pilot readiness

Requirements, acceptance criteria, test evidence, defects, retest status, site readiness, and open exceptions formed the go/no-go view.

04

Sponsor reporting and decisions

Weekly sponsor syncs converted delivery detail into schedule or sprint state, risk exposure, decisions required, and next-period priorities.

04 / Critical management episode

One city pilot exposed several distinct field failure modes.

A common product baseline kept one sponsor view while product defects and site or environmental adaptations followed separate owners, evidence, and acceptance paths.

Night

LED sign glare disrupted camera detection

Coordinated the cross-workstream issue until the technical team synchronized detection with the sign cycle, then linked the correction to retest evidence.

Wind

Camera displacement caused detection failure

Tracked the failure from field symptom to root cause and coordinated the mounting correction and validation path.

Winter

Cabinet survival required controlled adaptations

Heating, cold-start behaviour, enclosure protection, and maintenance requirements were managed as explicit adaptation packages with separate acceptance evidence.

Decision

Separate product defects from site adaptations

This preserved a clean sponsor view of what blocked the product baseline, what belonged to the installation or environment, and what was ready to move forward.

06 / Recruiter takeaway

Readiness becomes credible when it is explicit.

This case demonstrates technical implementation leadership across specialist workstreams, delivery methods, field constraints, QA evidence, sponsor decisions, and controlled acceptance—without confusing activity with readiness or coordination with engineering authorship.
